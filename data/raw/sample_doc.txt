The Nile is the longest river in Africa and has historically been
considered the longest river in the world. It flows northward through
eleven countries and empties into the Mediterranean Sea.

Ancient Egyptian agriculture depended on the Nile's annual flooding,
which deposited nutrient-rich silt along the riverbanks. Farmers grew
wheat, flax, and papyrus in this fertile soil.

The river has two major tributaries: the White Nile, which is the
primary stream, and the Blue Nile, which carries most of the water and
fertile sediment. They meet near Khartoum, Sudan.

Spanning South America, the Amazon River stands as the world's largest river by water volume. It flows eastward through countries like Peru and Colombia before cutting across Brazil to empty into the Atlantic Ocean. The local floodplains, known as varzea, receive a rich layer of sediment during the high-water season. This natural fertilization allows regional farmers to successfully cultivate crops like cassava, jute, and beans. The river's massive network is fed by thousands of waterways, with the Rio Negro and the Madeira River serving as its most prominent tributaries.

Winding entirely through China, the Yangtze River is recognized as the longest river in Asia. It originates high up in the Tibetan Plateau and journeys eastward to its mouth at the East China Sea near Shanghai. The surrounding basin functions as a massive agricultural hub, driving the intensive cultivation of rice, wheat, and oilseed crops. This vital waterway is also central to China's industrial transport and hydroelectric power generation. Its extensive system is supported by several major incoming rivers, including the Han, Jialing, and Min tributaries.

Carving through the central United States, the Mississippi-Missouri river system forms the largest watershed in North America. It flows predominantly southward, gathering water from dozens of states before emptying into the Gulf of Mexico via a sprawling delta. The fertile lands of the Mississippi Valley support a massive agricultural economy centered on corn, soybeans, and cotton. Additionally, the river serves as a commercial superhighway for shipping barges. Its main channel is heavily fed by the Missouri River from the west and the Ohio River from the east.

Flowing strictly northward into the Arctic Ocean, the Yenisei River serves as the central pillar of the great Siberian river systems. It starts in the mountains of Mongolia and cuts through the harsh landscapes of the Russian taiga and tundra. Because the basin suffers from extreme seasonal freezing, agricultural activities are limited to hardy root crops and livestock grazing in the southern valleys. The river is highly prized for its massive hydroelectric dams and timber rafting industries. Its primary flows come from the Angara River and the Lower Tunguska.

The Yellow River, or Huang He, flows through nine Chinese provinces and is celebrated as the cradle of ancient Chinese civilization. Originating in the Qinghai mountains, it follows a tortuous path to the Bohai Sea. It is famous for carrying immense loads of fine loess silt, which colors the water and creates exceptionally fertile plains but historically caused devastating floods. Local communities rely heavily on its waters to irrigate fields of millet, winter wheat, and corn. The river's midsection is joined by its two largest tributaries, the Wei and Fen rivers.

The Ob-Irtysh river system is a massive Siberian network that flows northward through western Russia into the Arctic Ocean's Gulf of Ob. It begins in the Altai Mountains and crosses the Vasyugan Swamp, one of the largest wetlands on Earth. Due to extensive permafrost across the region, farming is restricted to northern forestry and localized dairy farming in the south. The river remains a crucial transit route for supplying Russia's northern oil and gas fields. The long Irtysh River acts as its principal tributary, joining the Ob main stem at Khanty-Mansiysk.

Flowing through Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina, the Parana River is the second-longest river system in South America. It runs southward to merge with the Uruguay River, forming the massive Rio de la Plata estuary on the Atlantic coast. The river basin drains the highly productive Pampas region, where commercial farmers grow vast quantities of soy, maize, and wheat. It also provides a vital trade corridor for landlocked nations in the region. Its main upper branches are the Paraguay River and the Iguazu River, which is famous for its waterfalls.

The Congo River, formerly known as the Zaire, cuts through the heart of Africa and holds the title of the world's deepest recorded river. It flows through the planet's second-largest tropical rainforest, crossing the equator twice before emptying into the Atlantic Ocean. Along the riverbanks, local communities practice subsistence farming to grow staple crops like cassava, plantains, and yams. Because of its reliable, massive water volume and steep drops, the river has immense hydroelectric potential. Its largest tributaries are the Ubangi and Kasai rivers.

The Amur River establishes a long natural boundary between the Russian Far East and Northeastern China as it flows east into the Strait of Tartary. It moves through a diverse mix of dry steppes and dense temperate forests, freezing over completely during the bitter winter months. During the brief summer heat, the surrounding basin supports regional agriculture focused on soybeans, wheat, and sugar beets. The river is also globally recognized for its unique fish biodiversity, particularly sturgeon. Its main contributing rivers are the Songhua and the Zeya.
